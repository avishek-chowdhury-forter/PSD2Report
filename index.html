    </div>

    <div class="exec-body">

        <p>PSD2 reduced card fraud. That part worked. European card-not-present (CNP) fraud fell by <strong>12% in 2021</strong> following SCA enforcement (ECB Report on Card Fraud, 2021).         <p>But the regulation created a second problem that most organisations are still not measuring. When customers are sent through a 3DS authentication challenge, a significant proportion never come back. They abandon. This is not a compliance project. It is a revenue recovery opportunity critical to business growth.</p>

        <p>Across European merchants, five distinct levels of authentication maturity have emerged. Most are leaving significant revenue on the table. A small group are not. This report shows you which group you are in, and what closing the gap is worth.</p>

        <p style="margin-bottom:0;">The cost of a failed authentication is not just the transaction. It is the customer. One negative experience in the checkout flow can end a relationship that took years of marketing spend to build.</p>

      <h2 style="margin-bottom:10px;">The Compliance Comfort Zone</h2>

      <p class="section-sub">How PSD2 reshaped fraud prevention in European ecommerce</p>

    </div>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">PSD2 was built to modernise European payments and open the market to new players. Strong Customer Authentication was its answer to one specific problem: rising card-not-present (CNP) fraud, which had reached <strong style="color:var(--black);">€1.87 billion in 2019</strong> (ECB Report on Card Fraud, 2020) and accounted for around 84% of total card fraud value. Something had to change.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">By requiring customers to verify identity using two factors, regulators created a meaningful barrier to card-not-present fraud. After a phased rollout, full enforcement landed across most EEA markets by end of 2020, with some countries including Italy, Germany, and Ireland extending into 2021, and the UK following in March 2022. Once in place, it worked. ECB data shows a <strong style="color:var(--black);">12% decline in CNP fraud in 2021</strong> (ECB Report on Card Fraud, 2021). By 2024, card fraud was running at <strong style="color:var(--black);">ten times the rate</strong> in markets without equivalent SCA protections compared to within the EEA (Mastercard EMV 3DS programme data, 2024).</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">SCA enforcement did not eliminate fraud. It displaced it. As card payments became harder to exploit, fraudsters moved elsewhere. The EBA and ECB's 2025 Joint Payment Fraud Report documents a rise in social engineering schemes, impersonation scams, and authorised push payment fraud in the years following SCA enforcement (EBA/ECB Joint Report on Payment Fraud, 2025). Forter's own network data tells a similar story: since PSD2 came into full effect across Europe, ATO attempts across its merchant base have risen <strong style="color:var(--black);">180%</strong>, gift card fraud <strong style="color:var(--black);">230%</strong>, and refund abuse including false INR claims more than <strong style="color:var(--black);">200%</strong>. PSD2 solved the problem it was designed to solve. The fraud landscape it created is a different conversation, and an important one. The problem this report addresses is not the fraud PSD2 pushed elsewhere. It is the revenue it cost merchants at the moment of authentication.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">And so a reasonable default settled in. Authentication was handled. Card fraud was down. Remaining checkout losses were attributed to the usual suspects. Compliance was ticked. The work felt done.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:40px;">It was not. Because something else was happening that the standard reporting could not see. It is still happening. This report shows you how far up that ladder your business sits, and what closing the gap is worth.</p>

    <div style="margin: 40px 0; padding: 24px 32px; border-left: 4px solid var(--blue); background: var(--blue-light); border-radius: 0 12px 12px 0;">

      <p style="font-size: 17px; font-weight: 600; color: var(--black); line-height: 1.6; margin: 0;">The merchants operating at the highest levels of authentication maturity did not wait for the next compliance deadline. They built a capability their competitors had not thought to measure.</p>

    </div>

    <div style="background:var(--blue-light);border-radius:16px;padding:32px 36px;width:100%;">

      <h3 style="font-size:18px;margin-bottom:12px;">The liability shift</h3>

      <p style="color:var(--muted);font-size:15px;margin:0;">When a transaction completes 3DS, fraud liability shifts from the merchant to the card issuer. That is a genuine benefit. But it created a measurement blind spot: the impact of authentication was measured only in terms of fraud prevention, and did not account for customers lost during the authentication process itself.</p>

      <h2 style="margin-bottom:10px;">The Revenue Leak Revealed</h2>

      <p class="section-sub">How authentication friction erodes checkout conversion</p>

    </div>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">Every day, genuine customers abandon purchases during authentication. Not because they changed their mind. Not because of fraud. Because the process interrupted them at the worst possible moment and they did not come back.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">A customer has spent time on your site. They have made a decision. They have added items to their basket, entered their card details, and clicked pay. They want this purchase. A screen appears from their bank. They need to open a banking app, find a one-time code, or navigate a redirect that looks nothing like your brand experience. On mobile, where over half of your transactions originate, it can be genuinely disruptive.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:40px;">Sometimes families share cards and the person who gets the authentication request is not the one trying to buy. Sometimes the customer is not near their phone. Sometimes the OTP never arrives because their number is out of date at the bank. There are a dozen other reasons a genuine customer does not complete the challenge. None of them are fraud. You almost certainly never know they were there.</p>

    <h3 style="margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;">Two types of loss, both hidden in standard reporting</h3>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:16px;">The drop-off has two distinct causes, and they require different fixes. The first is <strong style="color:var(--black);">customer abandonment</strong>: the customer sees the authentication challenge and gives up. The flow is too slow, too confusing, too unfamiliar. An experience and trust problem: better UX, push notifications, faster flows. The second is <strong style="color:var(--black);">technical failure</strong>: the authentication request times out, the redirect breaks, or the SDK fails on a specific device. An integration and infrastructure problem: flow rebuilds and cross-device testing.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:16px;">Both result in the same outcome: the transaction never reaches authorisation. The Merchant Risk Council has documented that these transactions are categorised as checkout abandonment in most PSP reporting, indistinguishable from a customer who decided not to buy. When retries are attempted, they typically repeat the same authentication flow with no change in outcome. The underlying issue — whether an issuer policy, a broken redirect, or a customer who has already left — remains invisible.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:40px;">The merchants who have closed this gap share one characteristic: they stopped measuring authentication as a fraud metric and started measuring it as a revenue metric. Most of their competitors still have not made that switch.</p>

    <div class="quote-card" style="margin-bottom:48px;">

      <p class="quote-text">"Managing fraud is not just about blocking fraudsters. It's about ensuring your genuine customers aren't wrongly flagged or inconvenienced."</p>

      <div class="quote-attr-name">Galit Shani-Michel</div>

      <div class="quote-attr-title">VP Payments, Forter</div>

    </div>

    <h3 style="margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;">The scale of the hidden loss</h3>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">In France and Spain, 3DS abandonment rates exceeded 40% in the early days of SCA rollout. European merchants typically see a 2–3.5% conversion decline from poorly implemented authentication. Early projections estimated €57 billion in checkout abandonment losses across Europe in year one of SCA enforcement. Markets have evolved since then. Stripe's analysis of regulated markets shows French frictionless authentication rose 40% in H1 2024, as more transactions qualified for exemptions and low-risk treatment (Stripe, 2024). The direction of travel is positive. But improvement is not the same as solved, and the gap between markets that have invested in authentication infrastructure and those that have not remains large.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:40px;">Global benchmarking data across 37 countries, including both regulated and unregulated markets, shows a 3DS challenge success rate of just 79% worldwide (Mastercard EMV 3DS programme data): nearly 1 in 4 customers who are challenged do not complete it. Separately, only 6 in 10 customers experience a fully frictionless journey, meaning they never encounter a challenge at all. In the UK, overall authentication success rates reach 93%, the highest in Europe, reflecting both a high frictionless rate and stronger challenge completion. In Germany, based on Forter's own network data, challenge-specific success falls to 49%. In Spain, 52%. In Italy, 54%.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">None of this friction is inevitable. High authentication rates do not damage conversion. Poor authentication implementation does.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:40px;">The impact is not evenly distributed across transaction values. Abandonment rates on transactions above €100 are 8–15 percentage points higher than on low-value transactions, across major European issuers in Forter's network.</p>

    <h3 style="margin-bottom:8px;font-size:20px;">Complete rate — the number you should be tracking</h3>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);"><strong style="color:var(--black);">Standard authorisation rate</strong> = successful authorisations divided by transactions submitted for authorisation. <strong style="color:var(--black);">Complete rate</strong> = successful transactions divided by all transaction attempts, including those abandoned or failed during authentication. This is where the biggest opportunity cost lies. Most merchants track only the first.</p>

      <h2 style="margin-bottom:10px;">Why We Don't See the Leak</h2>

      <p class="section-sub">The authentication black box inside the payments ecosystem</p>

    </div>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">If the problem is real and measurable, why do so few merchants know it is happening to them?</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">Authentication sits across four distinct parties: the merchant, the payment service provider (PSP), the card network, and the issuer. Each plays a role. None has a complete view. The issuer's Access Control Server makes the actual decision: frictionless flow, challenge, or decline. The merchant cannot see why challenges are triggered for specific customers, why one issuer accepts exemption requests and another systematically rejects them, or what is happening inside an individual bank's authentication infrastructure.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:32px;">Soft decline codes are generic. They confirm a transaction was declined for authentication reasons. They do not tell you whether the issuer has a blanket policy against your transaction profile, whether its challenge flow breaks on mobile, whether there is a specific BIN range where your completion rate consistently collapses, or whether the customer themselves dropped out through frustration or confusion despite the technical flow working correctly. Most PSPs do not surface this level of detail in standard reporting, but merchants who ask for it directly can often access more granular breakdowns. The barrier is usually awareness, not availability. What that data will show you is the scale of the problem. What it will not show you is the issuer-level, real-time picture you need to do something about it.</p>

      <div class="psp-q-header">

        <h3 class="psp-q-title">Five questions to ask your PSP today</h3>

        <p class="psp-q-sub">Most merchants have never asked their PSP for this level of detail. Start here.</p>

    <div class="quote-card" style="margin-bottom:48px;">

      <p class="quote-text">Once 3DS was configured with the PSP, nobody wanted to touch it. Nobody was entirely sure what was happening inside it. And so it ran, in the background, unmonitored and unoptimised.</p>

    </div>

    <h3 style="margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;">What the PSP is not showing you</h3>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">PSPs have limited incentive to surface authentication performance problems. They process transactions. They do not own your conversion rate. If customers are dropping off during authentication, those transactions never reach the authorisation layer. They never appear in the metrics the PSP reports to you. The data looks clean. Revenue is leaving and you cannot see why.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">PSPs also charge per 3DS authentication attempt regardless of outcome, and have an incentive to maintain low fraud rates for their card network standing. Neither of those things aligns with reducing unnecessary challenges for merchants. This is compounded by the fact that many PSPs both control the 3DS execution and produce the reporting on it. They are marking their own homework. When merchants do push for answers, PSPs can conveniently point to the issuing bank. After all, it is the issuer's Access Control Server making the final decision. The issuer is inaccessible to the merchant. The PSP has plausible deniability. And in many cases the PSP genuinely lacks the granular data to differentiate between a timeout, a protocol failure, and a customer who simply gave up.</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:20px;">Most teams can quote their fraud loss and chargeback rates to the decimal. Almost none have an equally rigorous view of the good revenue they are turning away. Merchants lose an estimated <strong style="color:var(--black);">13 times more revenue</strong> to false declines and unnecessary friction than they lose to actual fraud (Forter/Carat false decline research). According to MRC data, merchants reject around 6% of all e-commerce orders, with between 2% and 10% of those belonging to legitimate customers (MRC Global Payments and Fraud Report, 2024).</p>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);">The tools merchants rely on were designed to stop fraud and process payments. None of them were designed to answer the question that actually drives revenue recovery: given everything known about this customer and this issuer, right now, what is the optimal path through authentication?</p>

    <h3 style="margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;">Abandonment versus technical failure: two different fixes</h3>

    <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:16px;">In Germany and Italy, the data separates into two distinct problems.     <p style="color:var(--muted);margin-bottom:16px;">The technical failure rate reflects a structural issue common to traditional German and Italian issuers. Many still run redirect-based 3DS architectures that push the customer out of the merchant environment to a bank-hosted page. These flows were designed for desktop browsers. On mobile, in-app browsers, or under weak signal conditions, they break. UK issuers largely eliminated this problem by investing in SDK-based and app-to-app flows during their extended SCA implementation window.</p>
